# Ownership STAR

## Prompt pattern
"Tell me about a time you took ownership of something outside your scope."
"Describe a problem no one else was addressing that you picked up."
"When have you gone beyond what was asked of you?"

## STAR structure
**Situation**: the gap — something broken or underinvested that wasn't formally anyone's responsibility.
**Task**: why you decided to own it (and how you got alignment to do so).
**Action**: scoping, communication, execution — without abandoning your actual job.
**Result**: the gap closed + the ongoing ownership model after.

## Senior signals
- "Ownership" doesn't mean "hero mode." It means making the invisible visible and deciding.
- Show that you got buy-in from your manager before spending large amounts of time.
- Name a tradeoff: what you said no to in order to say yes to this.
- End with a durable outcome — ongoing ownership handed off or institutionalized, not just a one-time fix.
- Avoid framing yourself as the only one who cared. It reads as martyrdom.

## Template 1: Owning a cross-team reliability gap

**Situation**: <SYSTEM, e.g. shared authentication service> was causing intermittent failures across <N DEPENDENT SERVICES>. No team formally owned it — it had been inherited from a previous reorg. Each dependent team would file a ticket, nothing would happen for weeks, and everyone routed around the problem with retries and workarounds.

**Task**: Decide whether to keep routing around it or to take it on. I was a senior IC on one of the dependent teams, not on the auth platform team.

**Action**:
1. Aligned with my EM first: "I want to spend ~20% of my time on this for a month. Here's why I think it's worth it, here's what I'll drop from my current plate." Got agreement in writing.
2. Got the current code owners — an under-resourced platform team — on a call. Offered to do the work in their codebase following their review process, not to take over ownership.
3. Triaged the top 5 failure modes from <N MONTHS> of incident data. Fixed the two with the highest impact-to-effort ratio in <TIME>.
4. Wrote a runbook for the next 3 failure modes so on-call from any team could handle them without paging the platform team.
5. Explicitly rejected the temptation to rewrite the service. My job was to stabilize it, not to own it long-term.

**Result**: Related incidents dropped <OUTCOME PERCENT>. Platform team used the stabilization window to hire, and a quarter later formally owned the service again with a proper on-call rotation. My team's velocity improved because we stopped fighting retries.

**Senior quote**: "I wasn't trying to become the new owner. I was trying to buy the real owners enough breathing room to exist."

## Template 2: Owning quality when no one was

**Situation**: Team had been shipping fast but quality signals were slipping: <SYMPTOM 1, e.g. flaky tests ignored>, <SYMPTOM 2, e.g. code coverage trending down>, <SYMPTOM 3, e.g. bugs caught in prod that should have been caught earlier>. No one was explicitly responsible for quality — the EM and PM were focused on delivery.

**Task**: Bring quality concerns to a team-wide decision rather than either (a) letting the drift continue or (b) becoming the team's lone quality nag.

**Action**:
1. Instead of complaining in retro, pulled one month of data: flaky test rate, escaped bugs, time spent on hotfixes. Presented numbers, not vibes.
2. Proposed a small, time-boxed experiment: <PROPOSAL, e.g. a quality budget — one engineer per sprint rotates on "quality duty">. Framed as reversible — "try for 4 sprints, measure, kill if it's not working."
3. Volunteered to take the first rotation so the cost was visible to me before I asked anyone else to take it.
4. Defined success metrics up front with the team: flaky test rate < X, escaped bugs < Y, time-on-hotfix < Z.
5. Ran the rotation, documented what worked and what didn't, brought data back to the team at the 4-sprint checkpoint.

**Result**: Flaky test rate dropped from <BEFORE> to <AFTER>. Escaped bugs dropped <PERCENT>. Team voted to keep the rotation permanently. I handed off the rotation structure — I didn't make myself the permanent owner.

**Senior quote**: "Ownership isn't doing the work forever. It's getting the structure in place so someone else can."

## Template 3: Owning a decision nobody wanted to make

**Situation**: Team was sitting on a legacy <SYSTEM / SERVICE> that was costing time and money but was still generating <SMALL VALUE, e.g. some low-volume revenue / a few active customers>. Nobody wanted to be the one to sunset it because the revenue wasn't zero. The decision had been deferred for <TIME, e.g. 18 months>.

**Task**: Force the decision — either commit to maintaining it properly or deprecate it. Drift was costing more than either path would.

**Action**:
1. Built the actual numbers: maintenance cost (on-call time, security patch overhead, dependency drag) vs the actual revenue and strategic value. It was embarrassingly one-sided — cost was <N>x the value.
2. Took the case to my EM and the PM together. Proposed two options with dated decision points: (a) invest <N engineer-months> to modernize, or (b) sunset over <TIME> with a clear customer communication plan.
3. Wrote the customer communication draft for option (b) so the decision felt concrete, not hypothetical.
4. Acknowledged the uncomfortable truth out loud — "nobody wanted to be the one to say this, but deferring is already a choice and it's the most expensive one."
5. Made the decision a joint EM + PM + me decision, in writing, so no single person was alone on it.

**Result**: Team chose to sunset. Customers migrated over <TIME>. Reclaimed <N engineer-months/year> of ongoing maintenance. One of the sunset customers ended up upgrading to the modern product, which was a better fit for them anyway.

**Senior quote**: "The decision felt unkind until I realized that letting it drift was the unkindness. I was protecting people from a hard conversation that cost more than the conversation would have."

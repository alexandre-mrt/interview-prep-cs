# Conflict STAR

## Prompt pattern
"Tell me about a conflict with a coworker."
"Describe a time you disagreed with a peer on a technical decision."
"When have you had to work with someone difficult?"

## STAR structure
**Situation**: the decision at stake + why both sides had merit.
**Task**: your specific role in resolving it.
**Action**: understand their reasoning, identify the shared goal, propose a tiebreaker, commit to the outcome.
**Result**: decision made + relationship preserved. Optionally, updated view.

## Senior signals
- Frame it as a technical disagreement, not a personality clash.
- Never cast the other person as the villain.
- Best stories end with "they were partially right and I updated my view."
- Propose data-driven tiebreakers: prototype, metric, spike.
- Name the working relationship after the conflict — show it survived.

## Template 1: Technical disagreement on architecture

**Situation**: <PEER, e.g. senior engineer on the same team> and I disagreed on <DECISION, e.g. whether to use <TECH A> or <TECH B> for <SYSTEM>>. The stakes were <STAKES, e.g. 6 months of engineering time and ~$50K/month infra cost>. Both options had legitimate tradeoffs: <THEIR POSITION> vs <MY POSITION>.

**Task**: Reach a decision the team could commit to, within <TIMELINE, e.g. 2 weeks>, without either of us overriding the other.

**Action**:
1. Booked a 1:1 with <PEER> specifically to understand their reasoning — not to argue my case. Asked "what's the failure mode you're most worried about?"
2. Discovered their real concern was <UNDERLYING CONCERN, e.g. operational complexity at 3am>, which I had underweighted.
3. Proposed a <TIEBREAKER, e.g. 2-week spike> where we'd prototype both approaches against <CRITERIA, e.g. a realistic load test + a simulated oncall scenario>.
4. Co-wrote the evaluation criteria with <PEER> before the spike started so neither of us could move the goalposts.
5. Spike showed <RESULT, e.g. their approach was better on ops, mine was better on latency>. We chose <CHOICE> and documented the tradeoff in an <ADR, e.g. architecture decision record>.

**Result**: Decision shipped in <TIME>. <METRIC, e.g. system ran at p99 target with zero ops escalations in first quarter>. <PEER> and I have since collaborated on <FOLLOW-UP PROJECT>.

**Senior quote**: "I went into the 1:1 to understand, not to win. That's usually where the real answer is hiding."

## Template 2: Disagreement with a product manager on scope

**Situation**: <PM> wanted to ship <FEATURE> by <DATE> with <SCOPE>. I believed the timeline required cutting <ITEM, e.g. the migration path for existing users>, which I considered non-negotiable for trust reasons.

**Task**: Advocate for the constraint without blocking the launch — PM had legitimate business pressure from <SOURCE, e.g. a customer commitment>.

**Action**:
1. Restated the PM's ask and the business driver in writing to confirm I understood — avoided arguing against a straw man.
2. Quantified the risk of cutting the migration path: <RISK, e.g. estimated 15% of existing users would see broken state, support load +N tickets/week>.
3. Offered <N ALTERNATIVES, e.g. a feature flag for phased rollout, a reduced-scope v1 that kept migration safe, a 2-week extension with clear scope>.
4. Made the tradeoff a decision for <PM + EM> together, in writing, rather than relitigating it in standup.
5. Committed to whichever path was chosen, and pre-wrote the rollback plan for the riskier option.

**Result**: Team chose <OUTCOME, e.g. the phased rollout>. Shipped <TIMING, e.g. one week late> with <METRIC, e.g. zero migration-related incidents>. The phased rollout became our default pattern for risky migrations.

**Senior quote**: "I wasn't trying to stop the launch. I was trying to make sure we could reverse it if we were wrong."

## Template 3: Conflict with a skip-level / senior stakeholder

**Situation**: <STAKEHOLDER, e.g. director from an adjacent org> pushed hard for <REQUEST, e.g. a custom integration> on a timeline that would have derailed our quarter. My EM was hesitant to push back.

**Task**: Surface the tradeoff clearly without damaging the cross-org relationship or putting my EM in a bad spot.

**Action**:
1. Got on the phone (not Slack) with <STAKEHOLDER> to understand the underlying need. The custom ask was one way of solving <REAL PROBLEM, e.g. latency for their enterprise customer>.
2. Proposed an alternative that solved their real problem without requiring <ORIGINAL ASK>: <ALTERNATIVE, e.g. a config change in our existing API>.
3. Brought the alternative to my EM so they could represent it to the director — I didn't go around my EM.
4. Agreed with <STAKEHOLDER> on a joint success metric so the relationship didn't become transactional.

**Result**: We solved their problem in <TIME, e.g. 3 days instead of 6 weeks>. <STAKEHOLDER> has since brought <N OTHER REQUESTS> to our team first, because the collaboration worked. Relationship improved, not degraded.

**Senior quote**: "Their ask was a solution. My job was to find what they were really trying to solve."
